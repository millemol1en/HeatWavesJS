Jocelyn Bell & CP-1919

The origins of the art works inspiration goes back to 1967, where the young astrophysist Jocelyn Bell from the University of Cambridge, detected pulsars radio waves from a neutron star. Over 978.5 light years away, the electromagnetic radiation emitted from its poles was recorded and with successive 1.337 second intervals between each pulse, the pulsars were stacked on top of each other to create one of the worlds first scientific data visualization.

Joy Division - Unknown Pleasure

After having seen it in the Cambridge Encyclopedia of Astronomy, the post-punk band known as Joy Division, would use it for their debut album Unknown Pleasures, released in 1979. The album's skyrocketing success had a huge impact not only for the band but also for the album's cover, making it become synonymous with youthful rebelliousness, post-modernistic art and revolutionary technology. The art itself would later invoke Joy Division to create the song, "Transmissions" in 1979, after the release of Unknown Pleasures.

The Connection

Like Jocelyn's work, scientific data is used as the core building block - and like Joy Division, the topic which our piece deals with, Climate Change, is often associated with the rebellious nature of the youth.

Taking inspiration from these 2 stories and what they each represent, our project aims to create a new perspective on the data that has been represented as a graph 1 million times over.

The piece takes on 2 different views, one which offers the raw data and interaction along with it as well as one where it is simply the artistic representation of the piece by itself.
